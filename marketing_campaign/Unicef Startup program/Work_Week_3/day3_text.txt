Revenue Model vs Business Model DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 BUSINESS MODEL How you CREATE, DELIVER and CAPTURE value overall Includes: target customers, value proposition, channels, key resources, cost structure — and revenue streams Answers: "How does your entire startup work?" Example: Farmerline connects farmers to agri-inputs + market data via mobile, with an agent distribution network, funded by B2B SaaS + donor grants REVENUE MODEL How specifically you EARN MONEY from your customers — the pricing mechanism A subset of the business model focused on the money flow: who pays, how much, how often Answers: "How does money actually move to your company?" Example: Farmerline charges NGOs a GHS 2,500/month SaaS fee AND takes a 3% commission on farmer input purchases through the platform Key insight: same product, different revenue model = completely different businesses with different economics, growth paths and investor attractiveness.
[Notes for Slide 6]:
PRESENTER NOTES — REVENUE VS BUSINESS MODEL
TIMING: 20 minutes | 9:20–9:40 AM

KEY TALKING POINTS:
1. Business model is the FULL picture; revenue model is ONE component — the pricing mechanism
2. You can have the same business model with DIFFERENT revenue models. Illustration:
   Two food delivery startups (same model) — one charges restaurants 15% commission, one charges consumers a monthly subscription. Very different unit economics and cash flow.
3. The revenue model choice drives: cash flow timing, customer behaviour, investor attractiveness, and regulatory obligations

GHANA CONTEXT:
In Ghana, B2C subscription models historically struggled due to low card penetration and irregular income cycles. MTN MoMo Auto-Debit has changed this — startups can now set up recurring mobile money payments, enabling subscription models at scale.

DISCUSSION QUESTION (3 min):
"Can you describe YOUR startup's revenue model in one sentence right now?" Take 2-3 volunteers. Most will describe their product, not their revenue mechanism — use this to reinforce the distinction.

Five Reasons Your Revenue Model Determines Survival DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 The same product with different revenue models = completely different businesses. Cash Flow Timing: Transaction models give you cash today. Subscription requires upfront investment but gives you predictable monthly recurring revenue. Customer Behaviour: Freemium requires 50x more users to convert 1 paying customer. Are your unit economics viable at the scale you can reach? Scalability Ceiling: A pure consulting model cannot scale without proportional headcount. A SaaS model can serve 10x customers with the same team.

Subscription vs Transaction — Core Trade-offs DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 SUBSCRIPTION MODEL Best for: SaaS / digital tools, ongoing value delivery, professional retainers Key metrics: MRR, ARR, Churn Rate, Lifetime Value Ghana advantage: MoMo Auto-Debit now enables recurring payments on feature phones Challenge: irregular cash flow of target users (farmers, SMEs) affects commitment Ghana example: Hubtel Merchant (GHS 50/month) | Business Ghana News premium TRANSACTION MODEL Best for: Marketplaces, mobile money services, e-commerce, infrequent high-value purchases Key metrics: GMV, Take Rate, Average Transaction Value, Transaction Frequency Ghana advantage: dominant model — informal economy comfort with pay-as-you-go Challenge: take rate compression — competition drives commission rates toward zero over time Ghana example: Jumia (8-20% commission) | Zeepay diaspora transfer fee + FX spread The fundamental question: does your customer have regular predictable cash to commit (subscription) or do they transact infrequently with variable cash (transaction)?

PRESENTER NOTES — SUBSCRIPTION VS TRANSACTION
TIMING: 25 minutes | 11:00–11:25 AM

KEY TALKING POINTS:
1. The key trade-off: subscription = predictable revenue but harder to acquire; transaction = easy to start but lumpy cash flow
2. For social impact startups: subscription models align better with SDG-reporting requirements (measurable ongoing engagement vs one-off transactions)
3. Subscription "lock-in" is NOT a dirty word — when the product delivers real value, switching costs benefit both parties

GHANA CONTEXT:
MoMo Auto-Debit (available via Hubtel API): startups can now set up recurring mobile money payments. This has been a game changer for B2C subscription models in Ghana. Before this API existed, subscription models effectively required a bank card.

Freemium — The Conversion Math You Must Know DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 Industry average free-to-paid conversion: 2-5%. To get 100 paying customers, you need 2,000-5,000 active free users. FREEMIUM WORKS WHEN: The free tier genuinely delivers value — not a crippled demo that frustrates users The paid tier has clear, compelling upgrade triggers that create obvious value Customer acquisition cost is LOW — freemium relies on viral/referral growth mechanisms The product has network effects — each user makes it more valuable for everyone FREEMIUM FAILS WHEN: Free users become expensive to serve without converting to paid tier The upgrade reason is weak — 'more features' is rarely compelling enough alone Target market is too small to build a large enough free user base for conversion math The product is a commodity — competitors copy your free tier, removing differentiation Ghana example — mPharma's pharmacy network: Free inventory management tools for pharmacists (genuinely valuable). Paid: premium analytics + procurement financing (compelling upgrade reason = access to capital). This converted the free tier into a B2B financial services revenue stream.
[Notes for Slide 11]:
PRESENTER NOTES — FREEMIUM
TIMING: 20 minutes | 11:25–11:45 AM

KEY TALKING POINTS:
1. Most participants OVERESTIMATE their free-to-paid conversion rate — challenge them: "Would YOU upgrade to paid if you were your own customer? What would you need to see?"
2. The dangerous freemium trap: giving too much away for free means users have no reason to pay. The free tier must be valuable but deliberately incomplete.
3. Freemium requires SCALE before it generates meaningful revenue — usually not viable in early-stage unless you have strong built-in virality
4. mPharma's Trojan horse: they gave free inventory management to capture market share, then used that footprint to offer procurement financing (their real high-margin product)

GHANA CONTEXT:
Freemium works well when the free tier captures MARKET SHARE in a fragmented sector first. Example opportunity: professional tools (legal tech, accounting, HR platforms) where free tier educates users and paid tier operationalises their work.

DISCUSSION QUESTION:
"If you were building a freemium product for smallholder farmers in Ghana, what would the free tier include — and what specific trigger would convert them to pay?"

CAC and LTV — Customer Unit Economics DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 CUSTOMER ACQUISITION COST (CAC) FORMULA: Total Sales & Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers Acquired in same period Include ALL costs: ads, salesperson time, events, referral fees, free trials, agent commissions Common mistake: forgetting founder time in sales — if you spend 50% of your time on sales, your salary is part of CAC Target: B2C digital = CAC < GHS 50; B2B SaaS = CAC < 3 months of contract value Ghana example: GHS 10,000/month marketing spend → 200 new paying users = CAC of GHS 50 LIFETIME VALUE (LTV) FORMULA: ARPU x Average Customer Lifespan — OR for subscription: ARPU ÷ Monthly Churn Rate The Golden Rule: LTV must be at least 3x CAC for a fundable unit economics model LTV:CAC ratios: < 1x = losing money, 1-3x = marginal, 3x+ = healthy, 5x+ = excellent Common mistake: using average subscription LENGTH instead of calculating from actual churn rate Ghana example: GHS 20/month ARPU, 2% monthly churn → LTV = 20/0.02 = GHS 1,000 → LTV:CAC = 20x Payback period = CAC ÷ Monthly Gross Profit per Customer. Target: under 12 months for most B2C startups.
[Notes for Slide 14]:
PRESENTER NOTES — CAC AND LTV
TIMING: 30 minutes | 1:15–1:45 PM

KEY TALKING POINTS:
1. These three numbers (CAC, LTV, Payback Period) are the most common investor interview questions for early-stage startups. Participants MUST be able to calculate and state their numbers confidently.
2. Walk through the Ghana example LIVE on whiteboard — make participants compute Month 2 and Month 3 from the example numbers
3. Mobile money transaction fees reduce effective ARPU — if your MoMo payment gateway charges 2%, subtract this from your ARPU calculation

FACILITATOR EXERCISE (10 min):
Ask participants to SILENTLY estimate their CAC and LTV for 3 minutes, then share with a table partner for 2 minutes. Then ask 2-3 to share with the room.
Most will have:
(a) No idea — needs immediate coaching
(b) Numbers that are wildly optimistic — needs calibration
(c) Rough estimates with clear assumptions — encourage them, refine together

GHANA CONTEXT:
High churn is a major challenge in Ghana's B2C digital market — many users are "price shoppers." If monthly churn is > 10%, an LTV:CAC ratio > 3x becomes very hard to achieve, which means the business is structurally difficult. This is not a death sentence — it is a signal to invest in retention before acquisition.

DISCUSSION QUESTION:
"If your LTV:CAC ratio is below 1x right now — what are your three levers to fix it? (Hint: you can reduce CAC, increase ARPU, or reduce churn — choose one to focus on first.)"

FACILITATION TIP:
This is a high-stakes moment — some participants may realise their current business is not financially viable. Frame this constructively: "Knowing your numbers is the first step to fixing them. Ignorance of your unit economics is what kills startups."

Revenue Forecasting:The Bottom-Up Method DAY 3  |  MODULE 3 Investors distrust top-down forecasts ("Ghana has 5M SMEs, we capture 1%"). Bottom-up forecasts from real sales capacity are credible. Step 1 Define your PAYING customer segment. Not 'all Ghanaians' — be specific: urban SME owners in Accra with smartphones and a monthly cash flow > GHS 2,000. Step 2 Estimate monthly ACQUISITION RATE based on your marketing plan. How many new paying customers can your current team actually convert per month? Step 3 Apply ARPU — your average monthly revenue per PAYING customer. Subtract transaction fees and refunds. Be conservative. Step 4 Apply CHURN — subtract customers lost each month. If you do not know your churn rate, assume 8-10% monthly for planning. Step 5 Build month-by-month: Starting customers + New customers acquired - Churned customers = Total x ARPU = Monthly Revenue.
[Notes for Slide 15]:
PRESENTER NOTES — REVENUE FORECASTING
TIMING: 15 minutes | 1:45–2:00 PM

KEY TALKING POINTS:
1. Walk through a live example: 50 customers in Month 1, acquire 30/month, 5% monthly churn, GHS 80 ARPU
   Month 1: 50 x 80 = GHS 4,000
   Month 6: ~200 customers x 80 = GHS 16,000
   Month 12: ~390 customers x 80 = GHS 31,200
   PAUSE: have participants calculate Month 3 with a partner (3 minutes)
2. The forecast is only as good as its assumptions — transparency about assumptions is a sign of founder maturity to investors
3. Being honest about a conservative forecast is more impressive than an unrealistic optimistic one

GHANA SEASONAL FACTORS:
- January-February: post-Christmas cash crunch — many SME customers spend conservatively
- August: school fees pressure — household discretionary spending drops
- October-December: harvest season for cocoa and maize — agricultural customers flush with cash
- Q4: mobile money system maintenance windows can affect transaction-based revenue

FACILITATION TIP:
If time allows, walk through the calculation LIVE on whiteboard. Ask: "What is your biggest uncertainty in this forecast?" The answer reveals the key assumptions that need immediate testing.

GHANA CASE STUDY Zeepay FinTech  |  Accra, Ghana  |  Founded 2014  |  Revenue: Transaction Fee + FX Spread Problem: 700,000+ Ghanaian diaspora (UK, US, Canada) sending money home faced 5-8% bank fees and 3-5 day delays. No solution focused on mobile wallet delivery in Ghana. Revenue Model: Blended: flat transaction fee (USD 2.99-4.99) + FX spread (0.5-1.5% margin over interbank rate). Both streams kick in on every single transfer. Competitive Edge: Delivery speed to MoMo wallet in under 60 seconds. Regulatory moat: first Ghanaian fintech with UK FCA + US MSB licences — took 18 months, impossible to fast-follow. Scale: Grew to process $1B+ annualised remittance volume across 50+ corridors. Series A from Mastercard. Ghana's Bank of Governor cited as model for inclusive fintech. Key Lesson: The pricing innovation was not the fee — it was delivery speed to mobile wallet. Revenue followed differentiation, not the other way around. DAY 3  |  MODULE 3
[Notes for Slide 16]:
PRESENTER NOTES — ZEEPAY CASE STUDY
TIMING: 30 minutes | 2:00–2:30 PM

TELL THE STORY:
Zeepay founder Andrew Takyi-Appiah identified a gap that banks and traditional remittance players overlooked: Ghana's 40%+ unbanked population could not receive bank wire transfers. But they all had MTN MoMo wallets. Zeepay built a "last mile" delivery system — connecting global payment rails to local mobile wallets.

KEY TEACHING POINTS:
1. The blended revenue model worked because: transaction fee covered basic operating costs; FX spread was the upside lever that scaled with volume
2. Regulatory moat: UK FCA approval took 18 months and significant capital. This created a real barrier that raised the cost of entry for any copycat — and signalled to enterprise clients that Zeepay was serious
3. Ghana diaspora remittance context: remittances are Ghana's second largest foreign exchange source (est. $4.5B in 2022). Even 1% market share = $45M/year in gross payments value

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS:
1. "Why couldn't a Ghanaian bank copy Zeepay's model immediately? What were Zeepay's real competitive moats beyond the technology?"
2. "How would you model Zeepay's LTV for a regular diaspora sender who remits $200/month? (Hint: calculate annual fees + FX spread x years retained)"

FACILITATOR TIP:
Use Zeepay as a pricing model example: the flat fee vs percentage model is a real pricing strategy decision. Flat fees are easier for customers to predict; percentage models scale better with large transfers.

PRESENTER NOTES — DAY 3 EXERCISE
TIMING: 35 minutes | 3:15–3:50 PM

FACILITATION STRUCTURE:
- 0-3 min: Read the brief aloud, clarify questions, confirm working format (individual or same teams as Day 2)
- 3-30 min: Working time — walk the room CONSTANTLY, intervene early when you see errors
- 30-35 min: Ask 3 teams to share their revenue model choice + LTV:CAC ratio (60 seconds each). Peers give 1 question each.

KEY COACHING INTERVENTIONS BY TASK:
- Task 1 (model selection): Most will default to "transaction fee" because it is familiar. Push: "Is there a subscription version of this? What would need to be true for subscribers to commit?"
- Task 3 (unit economics): Many will struggle with churn estimation. Help: "Of 10 customers you acquire today, how many do you genuinely believe will still be paying in 12 months? Start there."
- Task 4 (forecast): Watch for hockey-stick assumptions — if they project 0 customers now but 500 in Month 6, ask "HOW?" Make them explain the specific marketing actions that produce each new customer.
- Task 5 (risk): Most will write generic risks. Push for specificity: "My #1 risk is that MoMo Auto-Debit adoption is slower than assumed, which reduces my subscription conversion rate from 8% to 3%."

COMMON MISTAKES TO COACH:
- Setting prices too low from fear: "I'll charge GHS 5/month" — challenge: "At GHS 5/month you need 5,000 customers to reach GHS 25k MRR. Is that achievable in 12 months? Could you charge GHS 50/month to 500 customers instead?"
- Forgetting MNO transaction fees (2-2.5% of every MoMo payment) — these are real COGS that erode ARPU

FACILITATION TIP:
If teams are stuck on the forecast, try the reverse approach: "What monthly revenue do you need to be sustainable by Month 12? Work backwards from that target to figure out how many customers you need and at what ARPU."
